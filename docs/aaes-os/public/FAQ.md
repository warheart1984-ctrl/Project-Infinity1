# Version 1.0 Public FAQ

Constitutional Runtime - Public-Facing Explanation and Guidance
Status: Version 1.0 (Frozen Baseline)

## I. Introduction

This FAQ explains the Version 1.0 Constitutional Runtime, the governance architecture that defines how autonomous systems built on AAES-OS maintain continuity, safety, provenance, and long-term stability.

It is written for:

- contributors
- reviewers
- institutions
- researchers
- future stewards
- the general public

Version 1.0 is frozen, meaning its constitutional primitives cannot change without evidence-backed amendment.

## II. What Is the Constitutional Runtime?

The Constitutional Runtime is the Tier-1 governance layer of AAES-OS.

It defines:

- the system's foundational guarantees
- the invariant engines that enforce those guarantees
- the provenance and evidence rules
- the stewardship model
- the amendment process
- the continuity obligations

It is the constitution of the platform.

## III. Why Does the System Need a Constitution?

Autonomous systems evolve.

Without constitutional constraints, they drift.

Drift leads to:

- unpredictable behavior
- inconsistent guarantees
- loss of provenance
- unsafe authority expansion
- erosion of accountability
- fragmentation of governance

The Constitutional Runtime prevents drift by establishing immutable guarantees that govern all future evolution.

## IV. What Does "Version 1.0 Frozen" Mean?

It means:

- the constitutional primitives are locked
- the invariant engines are locked
- the stewardship map is locked
- the dependency ledger is locked
- the evidence record is locked

No changes can be made unless evidence demonstrates a constitutional deficiency.

This freeze ensures:

- long-term stability
- founder-independent governance
- external auditability
- reproducible evolution
- legal durability

## V. What Are Constitutional Primitives?

Constitutional primitives are the irreducible building blocks of the governance architecture.

Examples include:

- provenance rules
- evidence classification
- invariant engine definitions
- stewardship derivation logic
- continuity guarantees

They form the foundation upon which all higher-level governance is built.

## VI. What Are Invariant Engines?

Invariant engines enforce constitutional guarantees.

They ensure:

- provenance integrity
- dependency correctness
- continuity preservation
- authority containment
- reproducibility
- auditability

They are not policies. They are mechanisms.

They validate behavior, detect violations, and enforce guarantees.

## VII. What Is the Dependency Ledger?

The dependency ledger maps:

- each constitutional primitive
- the evidence that justifies it
- the relationships between primitives
- the stewardship assignments derived from those relationships
- the operational modules that depend on them

It is the spine of the constitution.

## VIII. What Is the Evidence Ledger?

The evidence ledger contains:

- the validated proofs supporting Version 1.0
- the provenance of those proofs
- the reproducibility results
- the cross-review confirmations

It demonstrates that Version 1.0 is proven to its release evidence threshold, not asserted.

## IX. What Is Stewardship?

Stewardship is the responsibility to:

- uphold the frozen constitution
- maintain provenance and evidence integrity
- validate operational evolution
- prevent drift
- initiate amendments only when evidence demands it

Stewards do not own the constitution. They maintain it.

## X. How Are Stewards Selected?

Stewardship is derived from:

- validated contributions
- dependency relationships
- evidence-anchored roles
- constitutional literacy
- demonstrated reliability

It is not assigned politically or socially.

It is derived structurally.

## XI. What Is "Validation Before Amendment"?

It is the core doctrine of Version 1.0:

We do not amend the constitution because we have new ideas.

We amend the constitution because the existing guarantees fail.

This ensures:

- constitutional stability
- evidence-first governance
- disciplined evolution
- protection against drift
- founder-independent continuity

## XII. When Can the Constitution Be Amended?

Only when all of the following are true:

1. A constitutional guarantee fails under validated conditions.
2. Operational evolution cannot fix the failure.
3. Evidence is complete, reproducible, and cross-reviewed.
4. The dependency ledger confirms a Tier-1 deficiency.
5. Stewards reach unanimous amendment consensus.

Amendments are rare and evidence-backed.

## XIII. What Is the Difference Between Constitutional and Operational Layers?

Constitutional Layer (Tier-1):

- frozen
- evidence-backed
- governs everything
- changes only through amendment

Operational Layer (Tier-2+):

- unfrozen
- evolves continuously
- implements constitutional guarantees
- can be modified freely as long as guarantees hold

New ideas belong in the operational layer unless evidence proves a constitutional deficiency.

## XIV. How Does Operational Evolution Work Under Version 1.0?

Operational evolution may:

- add new modules
- improve performance
- expand capabilities
- refine governance engines
- introduce new workflows

But it may not:

- violate constitutional guarantees
- bypass invariant engines
- alter provenance rules
- modify stewardship assignments
- change Tier-1 primitives

Operational evolution is the engine of progress.

The constitution is the anchor.

## XV. How Does the System Ensure Continuity?

Continuity is preserved through:

- invariant engines
- provenance rules
- dependency mapping
- stewardship derivation
- amendment gatekeeping
- the frozen baseline

This ensures the system remains stable across generations.

## XVI. What Happens If a Guarantee Fails?

Stewards must:

1. Validate the failure.
2. Attempt operational fixes.
3. Document evidence.
4. Conduct cross-review.
5. Determine whether the failure is constitutional.
6. Initiate amendment only if necessary.

Guarantee failures are treated with maximum seriousness.

## XVII. What Is the Role of Contributors?

Contributors may:

- build operational modules
- improve execution engines
- refine governance workflows
- propose evidence
- report stress signals
- participate in validation

Contributors do not modify the constitution directly.

## XVIII. How Can Institutions Audit Version 1.0?

Institutions may audit:

- the evidence ledger
- the dependency ledger
- the invariant engines
- the stewardship map
- the amendment rules
- the constitutional primitives

Version 1.0 is designed for external auditability.

## XIX. Why Freeze Version 1.0 Now?

Because:

- the primitives were validated
- the invariant engines were proven to the release threshold
- the evidence ledger was complete
- the stewardship map was derivable
- the guarantees held under stress
- the architecture demonstrated continuity

Version 1.0 is not perfect. It is proven to its freeze threshold.

## XX. Closing Statement

The Version 1.0 Constitutional Runtime is the foundation upon which governed autonomous systems will evolve.

It is stable, validated, and frozen: not to limit innovation, but to anchor it.

This FAQ is the public gateway into that architecture.
